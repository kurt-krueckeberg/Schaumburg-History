Overview


The Counts of Schaumburg, named after the castle of the same name above the Weser Valley near Rinteln, enter the stage of history in 1110 when King Lothar von Süpplingenburg gave the noble Adolf von der Schaumburg the county of Holstein. Under Adolf's successors, the Schaumburgs rose to become one of the most important families on the southern Baltic Sea. The common history of today's Schleswig-Holstein and the Schaumburg district is symbolically expressed in their plaques: Both use the nettle leaf in their coat of arms.


In the original lands on the central Weser, the Schaumburgs created a closed territory in conflict with the Counts of Roden, the Dukes of Saxony and the Bishops of Minden, which flourished under Count Ernst (ruled 1601-1622, Prince of the Reich since 1619 ) experienced. He created the legal and constitutional basis for the next centuries, founded the University of Rinteln, furnished the mausoleum in Stadthagen with the world-famous bronze sculptures of Adrian de Vries and turned the town of Bückeburg into a glamorous residence, so that it still bears his signature today. After the male line of the Counts of Holstein-Schaumburg died out in 1640, the Pinneberg estate was sold and the Weser County was divided. In the east of the county the offices of Lauenau with Mesmerode and Bokeloh, in the south the Bailiwick of Lachem and in the north of the spots Wiedensahl were drawn in by the dukes of Braunschweig and Lüneburg as settled fiefdoms. In addition, the county was divided into two halves according to the reports - only the coal mines remained under joint management:


  • The southern part of the county of Schaumburg with the towns of Oldendorf, Rinteln, Rodenberg and Sachsenhagen came under Hessian administration (county of Schaumburg Hessian portion). Until 1821, the government in Rinteln was under the direct control of the landgrave [a German count having a certain territorial jurisdiction] or, from 1803, the elector in Kassel, before the Hessian county of Schaumburg was assigned to the province of Lower Hesse [Niederhessen]. With the Prussian annexation of 1866, the district of Rinteln or county of Schaumburg became part of the province of Hesse-Nassau, and with the administrative reform of 1932 to the province of Hanover.
  • The northern part of the county of Schaumburg with the offices or places Arensburg, Bückeburg, Hagenburg, Stadthagen and Steinhude (county of Schaumburg Lippe with seat of government in Bückeburg) developed in the 18th century into the independent imperial county of Schaumburg-Lippe. It experienced its heyday in the second half of the 18th century under Count Wilhelm (r. 1748-1777), who turned his residence into a court of muses, and under the long reign of Georg Wilhelm (r. 1787 and 1807-1860, respectively), who assumed the title of prince in 1807. The state retained its independence after 1918, when the principality became the Free State of Schaumburg-Lippe, until the foundation of the state of Lower Saxony in 1946.

In the course of the administrative and territorial reform of Lower Saxony, the two districts of Grafschaft Schaumburg and Schaumburg-Lippe were merged to form the Schaumburg district in 1977, and thus roughly the boundaries of the old Grafschaft Schaumburg were re-established: Wiedensahl, known as the place of birth of Wilhelm Busch (1832-1908) was reassigned to the district in 1974 like half of the former Lauenau office. However, in the north part of the lake province with the Steinhuder Meer [sea] was separated in favor of the district of Hanover, in the south the municipality of Hessisch Oldendorf was separated in favor of the district of Hameln-Pyrmont.
